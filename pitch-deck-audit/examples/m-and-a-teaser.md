# Pitch deck lens — sell-side M&A blind teaser

**What this file is.** A filled sector lens for a sell-side M&A blind teaser — the short-form document an advisor distributes to potential buyers to test interest in an acquisition target whose identity is kept anonymous until a non-disclosure agreement is signed. The conventions of a blind teaser are the opposite of a fundraise deck: shorter, not longer; advisor-fronted, not founder-fronted; anonymized, not branded; no team slide, no competition slide. Most founders who receive or commission a teaser have no reference point for the format — this lens fills that gap.

**Source.** Structurally abstracted from an anonymized mid-market sell-side teaser, [mid-market M&A advisory firm, Brazil], Brazil. The 5-slide structure, anonymized issuer card, advisor-fronted contact, Portuguese-first register, and sector-signal-only branding conventions are drawn from that document.

**How to use this file.** A teaser is read differently from a fundraise deck. Sections 1–3 reframe what this artifact is and isn't. Section 4 (cover and first page) carries more weight here than in any other sector lens — the cover of a teaser is the only impression management gets before the NDA gate. Section 8 (red flags) covers the confidentiality failures that typically get teasers pulled or get the issuer identified before NDAs are signed.

**This is a living document.** The advisor contact information (§3, §11) and the comparable transactions (§10) require updating each time the mandate is live. The anonymization in §1 must be re-checked every time a new version is produced — specifics accumulate across drafts.

**See also.** [`playbook.md`](../playbook.md) §§1, 35 for methodology context. [`lens_template.md`](../lens_template.md) for the blank schema.

## 1. Company identity

```
Codename: [PROJECT NAME — a neutral codename unrelated to the company's sector,
  size, geography, or brand. "Project Atlas," "Project Horizon," "Project Cerrado."
  Avoid names that hint at the business: "Project Credit," "Project Farm,"
  "Project Bank" all leak sector signal.]
Sector signal (what can be disclosed without identifying the issuer):
  - Sector: [broadly stated — e.g., "financial technology," "agricultural services,"
    "healthcare infrastructure," "food & beverage"]
  - Geography: [country + broad region — e.g., "Brazil," "LATAM-headquartered operations"]
  - Scale: [revenue range in bracket form, not exact — e.g., "R$50–100M annual revenue"]
  - Business type: [2–3 words — "tech-enabled B2B services," "digital lending platform,"
    "agribusiness input distribution"]
What cannot appear before the NDA:
  - Company name or DBA
  - Brand name, domain, app name, or product name
  - Named customers, counterparties, or key employees
  - Precise revenue or EBITDA (use brackets)
  - Precise founding year or team names
  - Any identifier a buyer could use to search LinkedIn, Crunchbase, or CNPJ databases
    and identify the company before signing
```

**The issuer profile (for the advisor's internal use — not in the teaser).** Describe the actual company in full here so the advisor's team produces a consistent anonymization across every version. This section is in the advisor's working file, not in the distributed teaser.

## 2. Raise framing

A sell-side M&A teaser is not a fundraise instrument. The "raise framing" section in a blind teaser has different content from every other sector lens:

**What the teaser is communicating.** The issuer is exploring a transaction — sale of control, minority stake, strategic partnership, or merger — and the advisor has been mandated to identify interested parties. The teaser's only job is to cause the right buyers to sign the NDA and enter the process.

**Process framing (mandatory).** Every teaser should include a one-paragraph process description:

```
[PROJECT NAME] is being brought to market exclusively by [ADVISOR FIRM NAME].
Interested parties are invited to execute a confidentiality agreement before receiving
a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) with full company information.

Initial expressions of interest by: [DATE]
NDA execution deadline: [DATE]
CIM distribution to qualified parties: [DATE]
Initial bids: [DATE or TBD]
Management presentations: [DATE RANGE or TBD]

Contact: [ADVISOR NAME] — [EMAIL] — [PHONE]
This is a controlled process. All inquiries must be directed to the advisor.
Do not contact the company directly.
```

**Transaction type signal.** The teaser should signal whether this is 100% sale, controlling stake, minority investment, or open-to-structure — because different buyers self-select based on transaction type. Be specific: "the shareholder group is exploring a transaction involving 100% of the capital" is more useful than "exploring strategic alternatives."

## 3. Sector-specific slide arc

The blind teaser has the shortest arc of any artifact class in this kit. Five slides, no more.

| # | Slide / section | Content | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cover** | Codename + project description + advisor logo + confidentiality header | No company name. No company logo. No founding year. |
| 2 | **Company overview (anonymized)** | Sector signal, scale bracket (revenue range), brief business description, geography, 2–3 quantified metrics that don't identify the company | The equivalent of a fundraise deck's cover + problem + solution compressed to one page |
| 3 | **Market opportunity** | Why this sector is growing and why now is a good time to acquire in it; macro tailwinds; market sizing (sector-level, not company-specific) | This section is the only place a sell-side teaser resembles a fundraise deck — and only at the sector level |
| 4 | **Investment highlights** | 4–6 bullet points on what makes this company a compelling acquisition target — each anchored to a quantifiable fact (revenue bracket, client count bracket, growth rate, market position, technology ownership) | No company-identifying details. Use brackets for specific numbers: "R$[50–100]M ARR," "[>N]K clients," "[N]x revenue growth over [N] years." |
| 5 | **Process and contact** | The process description from §2. Advisor name, email, phone. Deadline calendar. "All inquiries to the advisor." | This must be the last slide. It is the only contact information in the document. |

**What is absent — and why.** No team slide (founders are identifiable). No competition slide (naming competitors identifies the market position). No product screenshots (the interface is often brand-specific). No financials beyond the bracket (exact figures enable reverse-engineering). The advisor fills the founder's role in terms of credibility — the advisor's reputation is what signals to buyers that this is a serious process.

## 4. Cover and tagline

The teaser cover is the only pre-NDA branding moment. Its job is to signal:

1. This is a professional process led by a credible advisor.
2. The sector is [X] and the scale is approximately [Y].
3. This is worth signing an NDA to learn more.

**Standard teaser cover structure:**

```
[ADVISOR FIRM LOGO — prominently placed]

CONFIDENTIAL

PROJECT [CODENAME]

[SECTOR SIGNAL — 3–4 words: "Financial Technology, Brazil" /
  "Agricultural Services, LATAM" / "Healthcare B2B, São Paulo"]

[TRANSACTION TYPE SIGNAL: "100% stake | Exclusive process" /
  "Controlling interest | Strategic partnership also considered"]

Prepared by [ADVISOR FIRM], [Month YEAR]

—

Strictly Confidential — For the Exclusive Use of the Addressee

This document contains confidential information. Recipients are prohibited from
copying, disclosing, or otherwise using this information without the express
written consent of [ADVISOR FIRM]. Any recipient interested in receiving further
information is required to execute a confidentiality agreement.
```

**Notes.** The sector signal on the cover should be broad enough to exclude competitors who should not know an M&A process is live, but specific enough to cause the right strategic buyers to self-select. "Financial Technology, Brazil" is correct granularity; "Digital ag-lending, southern Brazil" is too specific and too close to identification.

**Portuguese or English?** For Brazilian mid-market processes: Portuguese-first cover; English version available on request for international buyers. The process section (§2) must name whether bilingual support is available.

## 5. Sector framework

```
Underlying model: the M&A process has its own cycle — from mandate signing to LOI
  to CIM distribution to management presentations to final bids. The teaser is the
  pre-CIM market-test instrument.
Our position in it: the teaser is distributed at the beginning of the process to a
  wide list; the CIM is distributed to a narrowed list after NDA execution;
  management presentations to a narrower list; final bids to 2–3 qualified finalists
The sector-specific argument (for the "market opportunity" slide):
  - Describe the sector at the level of a vertical: "Brazilian financial technology
    is [N]× the size of [comparable market] and growing at [N]% annually"
  - Explain why strategic buyers in this sector are active acquirers right now
  - Do NOT describe the company's specific position in the sector
    (that's the CIM's job, not the teaser's)
What makes this acquisition compelling at the market level:
  - [Sector growth rate, market structure, consolidation dynamics, regulatory tailwinds]
  - Keep every claim at the sector level — not at the company level — until the NDA
    is signed
```

## 6. Cycle / positioning framework

For a sell-side M&A teaser, the "cycle / positioning framework" refers to the M&A market context:

```
Where we are in the M&A cycle: [describe current deal activity in this sector —
  consolidation wave, post-COVID recovery, PE dry powder seeking deployment, etc.]
Strategic buyer landscape: [types of acquirers active in this sector:
  - Brazilian strategic buyers (sector-adjacent companies looking to expand)
  - International strategics entering Brazil
  - PE / growth equity sponsors
  - Family offices with sector thesis]
Why now: [why this is a good time to bring this asset to market:
  - Sector-level: favorable valuations, strategic buyer appetite, regulatory tailwind
  - Company-level (for advisor's internal use, not in the teaser):
    founder liquidity need, succession planning, growth capital requirement]
Exclusivity language: if there is a reason why this process has a hard deadline
  (exclusive negotiation with a lead buyer, other capital event pending), surface
  it in the process section. Creates urgency without revealing confidential details.
```

## 7. Audit rubric (12 criteria, 1–5 scoring)

| # | Criterion | What 5/5 looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Codename is genuinely neutral | The codename does not hint at the sector, geography, founding story, or brand. Someone who has never heard of the company cannot use the codename to search for it. |
| 2 | Cover signals sector + scale + transaction type | A qualified buyer reading only the cover knows the broad sector, approximate scale, and type of transaction. |
| 3 | Advisor is prominently fronted | The advisor's name, logo, and contact appear on the cover and on the process slide. The advisor's reputation carries the credibility load the founder cannot carry anonymously. |
| 4 | Anonymization is consistent throughout | No slide leaks company-identifying information (name, brand, domain, key employee names, named customers, or precise metrics). Run a sweep of every page before distribution. |
| 5 | Revenue and metrics are bracketed | Specific figures appear as ranges: "R$[50–100]M" rather than "R$73.2M." Exact figures enable reverse-engineering. |
| 6 | Market opportunity is sector-level, not company-level | The market slide describes the sector's growth dynamics without naming the company's specific position. |
| 7 | Process calendar is populated and realistic | Deadlines for NDA, CIM distribution, initial bids, and management presentations are specific dates (or "TBD pending NDA execution rate") — not vague "coming soon." |
| 8 | Investment highlights are quantified | Each bullet has at least one bracketed metric: client count, revenue range, growth rate, market position. Qualitative-only bullets are weaker than quantitative ones. |
| 9 | "All inquiries to the advisor" is explicit | No founder contact information anywhere in the document. The advisor is the sole channel. |
| 10 | Transaction type is stated | 100% sale, controlling stake, minority, open-to-structure — the buyer needs to know which type of transaction is being explored to self-select correctly. |
| 11 | Confidentiality header is on every page | "Confidential — [Advisor] — Project [Codename]" in the footer of every slide. |
| 12 | Document length is sub-6 slides | More than 6 slides for a blind teaser means either the company has been identified (and this is a CIM, not a teaser) or the advisor is over-disclosing. |

## 8. Sector-specific red flags

```
1. Issuer-identifying details visible.
   Named employees (LinkedIn-searchable), named customers (Google-searchable),
   exact revenue figures (Receita Federal / B3-registerable for Brazilian entities),
   specific product names (Play Store / App Store searchable), domain names.
   Any one of these identifies the company before the NDA is signed.

2. Advisor contact buried on the last page or absent.
   If a buyer interested in the asset cannot find the advisor contact in 10 seconds,
   the teaser fails. The advisor contact should be on the cover AND the process slide.

3. Sub-6-slide promise broken.
   A "blind teaser" that is 15 pages is a CIM without the NDA protection. The longer
   the teaser, the more disclosure risk. If the material requires more than 6 pages
   to make the case for a buyer to sign an NDA, the problem is in the framing,
   not in the page count.

4. Team bios included.
   Any named team member with a searchable LinkedIn profile destroys anonymization.
   If team credentials are mentioned, use abstract descriptions only:
   "founding team with combined [N] years in [sector]; prior exits include [N] companies."

5. Exact metrics (not brackets).
   "R$73.2M revenue" allows a buyer to cross-reference against Receita Federal data
   or news searches. "R$[50–100]M revenue" does not.

6. Competition slide included.
   Naming competitors identifies the company's competitive position. In a sector with
   few players, naming even two competitors triangulates to the issuer.

7. Confidentiality header absent on any page.
   If a teaser page circulates without the confidentiality header, there is no
   contractual protection for the disclosure. Every page must carry it.

8. Process calendar missing deadlines.
   "We will be in touch soon" is not a process. Buyers evaluate multiple processes
   simultaneously; if this process doesn't have a calendar, it competes poorly
   against ones that do.
```

## 9. Investor objection bank

For a blind teaser, the "investor objections" are actually **buyer questions at the NDA stage** — what a qualified potential acquirer asks the advisor before signing:

**On the asset:**

```
1. Can you tell me more about what "financial technology" means in this context?
   Are we talking payments, lending, banking infrastructure?
2. What's the revenue quality — recurring revenue, transaction-based, project-based?
3. You've bracketed revenue at R$[50–100]M. Can you tell me if it's closer to the
   floor or the ceiling? We have a minimum threshold.
4. What's the EBITDA margin range?
```

**On the process:**

```
5. We're interested but our internal process takes [N] weeks. Can we extend the
   NDA deadline?
6. Who else is in the process? Are there other signed NDAs already?
7. Is the seller open to a minority investment or is this 100% sale only?
8. What does the seller's timeline look like? Is there a hard close date?
```

**On the transaction structure:**

```
9. Is an earn-out acceptable to the seller?
10. Is this process led by the founders or by a PE sponsor with a timeline?
```

## 10. Comparable raises

For a sell-side teaser, "comparable raises" means **recently closed transactions in the same sector at similar scale** — the evidence that comparable assets have transacted at certain multiples.

| Transaction | Type | Sector | Scale | Multiple | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Recent Brazilian fintech acquisition] | 100% sale | Fintech, Brazil | R$[X]M ARR | [N]x ARR / [N]x EBITDA | [Disclosed source, year] |
| [PE platform deal in adjacent sector] | Controlling stake | [Sector] | R$[X]M revenue | [N]x revenue | [Source] |
| [Strategic acquisition by Brazilian bank / incumbent] | 100% sale | Fintech / ag-tech | R$[X]M | [N]x | [Source] |

**What the comp table demonstrates.** For a sell-side teaser, the comp table belongs in the CIM, not the teaser itself. In the teaser, comparable transactions are mentioned in the "market opportunity" slide to establish that the sector sees active M&A at premium multiples — without naming specific figures that might reveal the expected price or anchor buyer negotiations.

In the advisor's working lens (this document), the comp table is fully populated so the advisor is prepared to discuss multiples the moment an interested buyer asks.

## 11. Draft slide for the sector-specific moment

The anonymized issuer card is the highest-value slide in the teaser — it must carry enough signal to cause the right buyers to self-select without carrying enough detail to identify the issuer.

```
Slide title: "Project [CODENAME] — company overview"

Layout: a two-column card

Left column — what can be disclosed:
  ● Sector: [Financial Technology — digital lending]
  ● Geography: Brazil, five states
  ● Founded: [N] years ago
  ● Business model: [technology-enabled credit origination]
  ● Customers: [>N]K active borrowers
  ● Revenue: R$[XX–YY]M (bracketed)
  ● Growth: [N]x over [N] years
  ● Profitability: [EBITDA-positive since YEAR / pre-EBITDA, path to profitability
    in [N] months]

Right column — the value proposition (sector-level framing):
  ● [Why this category is growing: 1–2 bullets at sector level, not company level]
  ● [What a strategic acquirer gains from this asset: distribution channel,
    technology, customer base, regulatory license, geographic footprint]
  ● [1 sentence on what the asset does that incumbents cannot do organically]

Bottom strip:
  [ADVISOR FIRM] | [CODENAME] | Strictly Confidential
  All inquiries: [Advisor name] — [email]
```

## 12. Style and voice guidance

- **Institutional-advisory register.** The teaser is authored by the advisor, not by the founder. The voice is "we are pleased to present this opportunity" not "we built this company." In Portuguese: "apresentamos exclusivamente em nome de nossos clientes."
- **Brackets for all company-specific metrics.** Every precise number that could identify the company appears in bracket form: "R$[50–100]M," "[>50]K clientes," "[N]x crescimento em [N] anos."
- **Passive construction for anonymization.** "The company has grown..." not "our client grew..." — the advisor is narrating about an unnamed company.
- **Short. Very short.** Every word in a teaser that doesn't cause a qualified buyer to sign an NDA is a word that adds disclosure risk. Delete with prejudice.
- **Bilingual for Brazilian mid-market.** Portuguese-first; English version on request for international buyers. Regulatory terms in Portuguese: "investidores estratégicos," "acordo de confidencialidade," "memorando de informações confidenciais."
- **No AI vocabulary.** Avoid: holistic, comprehensive, synergy, paradigm, impactful. In M&A advisory material, these words signal a template, not a bespoke analysis.

**Voice rules specific to sell-side teasers:**

```
- Never "disruptive" or "revolutionary" — M&A buyers are not buying a story;
  they are buying an asset with verifiable economics
- Always: "all inquiries to [advisor]" — the company should never appear
  as a contact
- "Acordo de confidencialidade" not "NDA" in Portuguese-first documents
- Never use first-person ("we," "our") unless it is the advisor speaking;
  the issuer's voice does not appear in the teaser
```

## 13. What Claude should NOT do

- Claude should **not identify or reveal the issuer.** If asked to produce a teaser for a named company, it should ask whether the company has been anonymized — if not, do not produce the teaser without anonymizing it first.
- Claude should **not include company-identifying details** (name, brand, key employees, customers, exact metrics) unless the founder explicitly confirms the NDA has been signed and these can be revealed in the CIM.
- Claude should **not produce a 15-page teaser** when asked for a blind teaser. The format is sub-6 slides. If the founder wants more disclosure than that allows, explain the distinction between a teaser (pre-NDA, short, anonymized) and a CIM (post-NDA, detailed, named).
- Claude should **not include a team slide or a competition slide** in a blind teaser. These are CIM sections. Including them in a teaser destroys anonymization.
- Claude should **not produce an English-only teaser** for a Brazilian mid-market process without confirming that no Portuguese version is needed. Brazilian institutional buyers and most Brazilian strategic acquirers conduct M&A processes in Portuguese.
- Claude should **not give fundraise-deck feedback on a teaser.** A teaser that "doesn't have a problem slide" or "lacks traction data" is not failing — it is correctly formatted as a teaser, not a fundraise deck. Audit it against the rubric in §7, not against the canonical 15-slide playbook arc.
