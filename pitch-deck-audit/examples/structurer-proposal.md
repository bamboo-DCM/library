# Pitch deck lens — structurer-to-counterparty commercial proposal

**What this file is.** A filled sector lens for a structurer-to-counterparty commercial proposal — the document a DCM firm, securitization desk, or structured-credit boutique produces when pitching an issuer (originator, corporate borrower, asset owner) to mandate their services for a structured transaction. The conventions are the opposite of a fundraise deck: no TAM, no team bios at length, no competition slide, no "problem / solution" narrative arc. The issuer already knows they want to access the capital markets or structure a credit vehicle. The structurer's proposal answers a different question: **why mandate this firm over the alternatives, and what does the transaction look like?**

**Sources.** Structurally abstracted from two sources: (1) a structurer-to-issuer proposal authored by [YOUR FIRM] for a transaction with [ISSUER] involving [ASSET CLASS] — all identifying details replaced with `[YOUR FIRM]`, `[ISSUER]`, and `[ASSET CLASS]` placeholders; do not reverse-engineer the specific counterparty from the structure. (2) The Percent / Percent A55 ABS proposal structure (publicly available memo; Source / Structure / Syndicate / Surveil framework) — cited with attribution.

**How to use this file.** Section 2 (transaction framing) and §11 (process diagram + term sheet) are the sections that make or break a structurer proposal. Sections 3 and 7 adjust for the fact that the slide arc and audit rubric are fundamentally different from a fundraise deck. Section 8 (red flags) covers the proposals that read as generic pitch material rather than bespoke analysis.

**This is a living document.** The term sheet economics in §2 and §11 must be updated before every new pitch — market spreads, regulatory requirements, and comparables change quarterly. The process diagram in §11 must match the specific transaction structure being proposed, not a generic template.

**See also.** [`playbook.md`](../playbook.md) §§1, 34 for methodology context. [`lens_template.md`](../lens_template.md) for the blank schema.

## 2. Raise framing

A structurer-to-counterparty proposal is not pitching investor equity. It is pitching a **commercial mandate**: the issuer is deciding which firm to engage as their structuring and placement agent for a specific transaction. The "raise framing" section covers:

**The transaction the structurer is proposing.**

```
Transaction type: [FIDC / CRA / CRI / ABS / debentures — fill per proposed instrument]
Issuer: [ISSUER] — [brief, non-identifying description]
Asset pool: [description of the receivables or assets being securitized or collateralized]
Target transaction size: R$[X]M / USD [X]M
Target investor yield: CDI + [N]% / IPCA + [N]% / fixed [N]%
Target rating: [if rated — agency and expected rating level]
Structural protections: [over-collateralization ratio, subordination, guarantees,
  events of default — see §11 for the full term sheet]
Target close: [QUARTER YEAR]
```

**The Percent / Percent A55 structural parallel (publicly cited).** Percent's ABS infrastructure uses a "Source / Structure / Syndicate / Surveil" four-step model (publicly available Percent A55 proposal): the structurer is not just placing an instrument but operating a lifecycle across origination, structuring, distribution, and ongoing surveillance. A proposal that explicitly describes all four workflow stages signals that the structurer understands post-close obligations, not just the initial placement.

**The fee structure.** Transparent fee disclosure in the proposal prevents mid-transaction disputes.

```
Structuring fee: [N]% of the principal amount, payable at close
Placement fee: [N]% of the amount distributed to investors
Annual monitoring / servicing fee (if applicable): [N]% of AUM or R$[X]/year
Success fee (if applicable): [describe trigger]
Total fee estimate for this transaction: R$[X]M at target size
```

## 3. Sector-specific slide arc

| # | Slide | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cover** | [YOUR FIRM] logo + [ISSUER] name (or abstract: "Proposta para [TRANSACTION TYPE] — [ASSET CLASS]") + date + confidentiality |
| 2 | **Structurer credentials** | Track record, regulatory standing, team tenure, relevant prior transactions (anonymized or with client permission), what differentiates this firm from comparable structurers |
| 3 | **Transaction overview** | What is being proposed: instrument type, size, asset pool, target investor base, target yield, target close |
| 4 | **Process diagram** | 3–5 step workflow showing who does what: originator / servicer / securitizadora / trustee / investors / regulators. This is the key slide — it replaces the "product" slide of a fundraise deck |
| 5 | **Term sheet** | Key economic terms: yield, coupon, subordination structure, events of default, garantias, tail risk, exclusivity clause, fee schedule. One page in the slide, with full term sheet in appendix |
| 6 | **Structural protections** | Events of default + remedies, guarantee package, coverage ratios, cash waterfall mechanics |
| 7 | **Placement strategy** | Which institutional investors will receive this instrument, why, and what historical interest has looked like for similar instruments. No TAM slide — this is an investor-base description, not market sizing |
| 8 | **Timeline** | Milestone calendar from mandate signing to close: structuring, CVM/ANBIMA filing, rating (if applicable), investor roadshow, subscription, close |
| 9 | **Exclusivity and tail** | Exclusivity terms (duration, carve-outs), tail clause (exclusivity window after mandate expiry), success-fee trigger |
| 10 | **Next steps** | What the structurer needs from the issuer to begin: data room access, management interview, LOI, engagement letter |

**Appendix (separate from main proposal deck):** Full term sheet, legal structure diagram, prior transaction comparables (with permission or anonymized), team biographies, firm regulatory authorizations, reference clients (with permission).

## 4. Cover and tagline

The cover of a structurer proposal is institutional. Unlike a fundraise deck, there is no "hook" tagline — the issuer already knows the structurer is pitching them.

**Standard cover structure:**

```
[YOUR FIRM LOGO]

PROPOSTA COMERCIAL CONFIDENCIAL

Estruturação e Coordenação de
[INSTRUMENT TYPE — e.g., Fundo de Investimento em Direitos Creditórios (FIDC)]
para [ISSUER NAME / "CONFIDENCIAL"]

Elaborado por [YOUR FIRM]
[Month, Year]

—

CONFIDENCIAL. Este material foi preparado exclusivamente para [ISSUER].
Sua reprodução ou divulgação a terceiros é proibida sem autorização expressa
de [YOUR FIRM].
```

**English version for international issuers or cross-border structures:**

```
[YOUR FIRM LOGO]

CONFIDENTIAL COMMERCIAL PROPOSAL

Structuring and Coordination of
[INSTRUMENT TYPE — e.g., Asset-Backed Securities (ABS) / FIDC]
for [ISSUER NAME]

Prepared by [YOUR FIRM]
[Month, Year]
```

  through a universal bank: [specific argument for this transaction]

## 7. Audit rubric (12 criteria, 1–5 scoring)

| # | Criterion | What 5/5 looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Process diagram is present and specific to this transaction | A 3–5 step diagram shows the exact flow of cash, assets, and instructions between originator, servicer, securitizadora, trustee, investors, and regulators for this specific instrument. Generic template diagrams score 2/5. |
| 2 | Term sheet is populated with this transaction's economics | Target yield, subordination structure, events of default, garantias, fee schedule — all specific to this deal, not a blank template. |
| 3 | Structural protections are named | Events of default, remedies, cash waterfall, over-collateralization ratio, guarantee package — all named and described, not "standard market terms." |
| 4 | Placement strategy is specific | Which investor categories this instrument will be distributed to, why, and what the evidence base is for their appetite (prior transactions in this asset class). Not "our extensive investor network." |
| 5 | Creditor hierarchy is explicit | Who gets paid first, second, and last in a stress scenario. Issuers often don't understand their own position in the waterfall until a structurer draws it clearly. |
| 6 | Exclusivity and tail terms are stated | Duration of exclusivity, any carve-outs, tail clause length. Issuers need to know what they're signing before they sign. |
| 7 | Fee structure is transparent | Structuring fee, placement fee, monitoring fee, success-fee trigger — all in one place, totalized for this transaction. |
| 8 | Timeline is realistic | Milestone calendar with specific dates or duration estimates from mandate signing to close. "4–6 weeks from mandate" is better than "as soon as possible." |
| 9 | Track record is specific, not generic | Prior transactions described with asset class, approximate size, and outcome — not "we have extensive experience in structured products." |
| 10 | Regulatory standing is confirmed | CVM 161 credential, ANBIMA registration, or other relevant authorization — named and current. |
| 11 | Comparable transactions are cited | 2–3 recent comparable transactions (same asset class, similar size) with pricing data — gives the issuer a benchmark for evaluating [YOUR FIRM]'s proposed economics. |
| 12 | No TAM, no team bios at length, no competition slide | A structurer proposal that includes market-size slides or company team bios in the main deck reads as a repurposed fundraise deck. These belong in the credentials appendix, not the proposal deck. |

## 8. Sector-specific red flags

```
1. TAM slide included in the main proposal.
   An issuer evaluating a structuring mandate does not need a market-size chart.
   They know the size of the market they operate in. A TAM slide in a structurer
   proposal signals the proposal was copied from a fundraise deck template.

2. Team bios at length in the main deck.
   Short credentials (one sentence per partner) belong on the cover or credentials slide.
   Full biographies belong in the appendix. A structurer whose main pitch is the team
   rather than the transaction mechanics has the wrong emphasis.

3. Generic process diagram.
   "Originator → Structurer → Investors" with no specifics is not a process diagram.
   A useful process diagram names the roles in this transaction: who is the securitizadora,
   who is the trustee (agente fiduciário), who is the credit analyst, what are the
   trigger points in the cash waterfall. Generic diagrams signal that the proposal
   was not customized for this issuer.

4. Events of default absent or summarized.
   "Standard market events of default" is not a disclosure. The issuer must understand
   what triggers a step-in right, what the remedy timeline looks like, and who has
   the authority to accelerate. Missing or summarized events of default create
   post-close disputes.

5. Garantias described without enforceability detail.
   "Full guarantee from the issuer" without naming the guarantor, the form of guarantee
   (fiança, aval, penhor, cessão fiduciária), and the legal opinion basis is
   not a guarantee — it is a promise. The issuer's counsel will ask these questions
   in diligence; the proposal should pre-answer them.

6. Placement strategy described as "broad investor base."
   Any structurer who has done transactions in this asset class can name two or three
   institutional investors who have historically purchased it. If the placement
   strategy is "we will approach the market broadly," the structurer is either
   new to this asset class or withholding information. Either is a flag.

7. Fee structure absent or deferred.
   "Fees to be discussed" is a standard evasion. Issuers comparing two proposals
   need to compare total cost of issuance. Deferring the fee structure means the issuer
   cannot evaluate the full cost until after they have spent time in the process.
   Transparent fee disclosure in the proposal is a competitive advantage, not a risk.

8. Exclusivity terms not surfaced in the proposal.
   If the engagement letter will ask for 90-day exclusivity and a 12-month tail clause,
   that belongs in the proposal, not sprung on the issuer when they're ready to sign.
   Surfacing exclusivity early signals confidence in the process, not desperation.
```

## 9. Investor objection bank

For a structurer proposal, the "investor objections" are actually **issuer objections** — the questions the issuer's CEO, CFO, or board will ask the structuring team before signing the engagement letter:

**On the transaction:**

```
1. What's the realistic pricing for this instrument in the current market?
   Give me a range, not a target.
2. Who exactly are the investors you'll approach? Name two or three.
3. What's the minimum viable transaction size? If we can't reach R$[X]M,
   is the structure still viable?
4. What's the worst-case scenario if the transaction doesn't close?
   What have we spent on fees and diligence?
```

**On the structurer:**

```
5. You've done [N] FIDCs. Have any of them had credit events?
   If yes, what happened?
6. What's the relationship between [YOUR FIRM] and the agente fiduciário
   you're proposing? Is there an independence question here?
7. Why is [YOUR FIRM] better for this transaction than [competing structurer
   or bank]? Give me one specific reason that is about our transaction,
   not about your general capabilities.
```

**On terms:**

```
8. Exclusivity for [N] days is a long time. What's the carve-out if
   a bank approaches us directly during the process?
9. The tail clause of [N] months feels long. What's the trigger?
10. Can we negotiate a deferred structuring fee tied to close rather than
    an upfront retainer?
```

**On timing:**

```
11. Your timeline says [N] months. What's the single biggest risk to that
    timeline and what do you need from us to stay on track?
12. CVM / ANBIMA filing is on [DATE]. What do you need from our legal team
    and by when?
```

## 10. Comparable raises

For a structurer proposal, comparable transactions serve a specific purpose: **price anchoring for the issuer.** The issuer needs to know that similar assets have transacted at similar yields in recent market conditions.

| Transaction | Asset class | Issuer type | Size | Pricing | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Recent comparable — anonymized if no permission] | FIDC / CRA / ABS | [originator type] | R$[X]M | CDI + [N]% | [Disclosed source or "proprietary transaction data, [YEAR]"] |
| [Percent A55 ABS (Percent Partners, publicly available)] | ABS (US market) | Private credit marketplace | USD[X]M | [Market rate at close] | Percent A55 public proposal documentation |
| [Brazilian market comparable] | [CRA / CRI / FIDC] | [sector] | R$[X]M | CDI + [N]% / IPCA + [N]% | [Source] |

**Notes on Percent A55 as a structural reference.** The Percent / Percent A55 ABS proposal (publicly available) is the clearest public example of the "Source / Structure / Syndicate / Surveil" four-stage lifecycle model. In a structurer proposal using a similar lifecycle framing, cite Percent's model explicitly as a precedent — institutional issuers who have seen the Percent model understand the workflow immediately. The Brazilian FIDC equivalent of the Syndicate stage is the agente fiduciário + distribution syndicate; the Surveil stage maps to the servicer and the trustee's ongoing obligations under CVM 175.

## 11. Draft slide for the sector-specific moment

The process diagram + term sheet is the combined slide that carries the most weight in a structurer proposal. It answers both "how does this work operationally?" and "what are the economics?" in one section.

```
Slide title: "Transaction structure — [TRANSACTION NAME / CODENAME]"

Part A — Process diagram:

[ISSUER / ORIGINATOR]
  → Assigns receivables to →
[SECURITIZADORA / SPE]
  → Issues cotas to →
[INVESTORS: senior cotas → institutional allocators / subordinated cotas → originator]

Cash flow waterfall:
  1. Issuer delivers receivables + [guarantee / aval / penhor] to securitizadora
  2. Securitizadora issues cotas (senior + subordinated)
  3. Investors fund cota purchase price to securitizadora
  4. Securitizadora disburses net proceeds to issuer
  5. Collected receivables → cash waterfall:
       (a) Senior cota coupon + principal
       (b) Fee payments (servicer, trustee, structurer)
       (c) Subordinated cota returns
  6. Events of default trigger → step-in rights → agente fiduciário assumes control

Part B — Key economic terms (summary):

| Term | Detail |
|---|---|
| Instrument | [FIDC / CRA / ABS / Debentures] |
| Total size | R$[X]M |
| Senior tranche | R$[X]M — CDI + [N]% — [RATING if applicable] |
| Subordinated tranche | R$[X]M — held by originator — first loss |
| Over-collateralization | [N]% minimum |
| Events of default | [list 3–5 key triggers] |
| Garantias | [fiança / aval / penhor / cessão fiduciária — specify form] |
| Exclusivity | [N] days from mandate signing |
| Tail | [N] months post-exclusivity for transactions introduced during period |
| Structuring fee | [N]% of principal |
| Placement fee | [N]% of placed amount |
| Target close | [QUARTER YEAR] |
```

## 12. Style and voice guidance

- **Process-first, credentials-second.** The issuer is evaluating whether [YOUR FIRM] understands their specific transaction. Lead with the transaction mechanics; move credentials to slide 2.
- **Numbers over narrative.** Every claim about market conditions, comparable transactions, and fee structures must be a number with a source and a date.
- **Bilingual.** Structurer proposals in Brazil are typically in Portuguese. International issuers or cross-border structures may require English or dual-language versions. Regulatory terms stay in Portuguese regardless: "agente fiduciário," "cota sênior," "cota subordinada," "cessão fiduciária," "fiança solidária."
- **Short.** A structurer proposal that is longer than 12 slides is competing with a CIM for the issuer's attention. Ten slides plus an appendix is the right shape. The appendix holds team bios, regulatory authorizations, full term sheet, and reference clients.
- **Professional register.** This is not a startup pitch. The issuer is not buying a vision — they are evaluating a counterparty for a complex transaction. The register is: precise, experienced, transactional.
- **No AI vocabulary.** Avoid: robust, comprehensive, holistic, synergy, impactful, paradigm. In a structured-finance context, these words signal generic marketing copy, not transaction expertise.

**Voice rules specific to structurer proposals:**

```
- Never "innovative solution" — describe the specific structural mechanism
- Never "broad investor network" — name two or three investor types
- "Agente fiduciário," "securitizadora," "coordenadora líder" — use the regulatory
  vocabulary, not the English translation (for Brazilian proposals)
- Always surface exclusivity and tail terms in the proposal; never defer to "TBD"
- Fee disclosure belongs in the proposal, not the engagement letter alone
```

## 13. What Claude should NOT do

- Claude should **not invent transaction economics** — yield targets, fee percentages, over-collateralization ratios — that haven't been provided by [YOUR FIRM]. Placeholder economics in a structurer proposal are a compliance and credibility risk.
- Claude should **not produce a generic process diagram** that doesn't name the roles in this specific transaction. Ask the structurer for the specific instrument, the proposed agente fiduciário, and the servicer structure before producing the process diagram.
- Claude should **not include a TAM slide or team bios at length** in the main proposal deck. These belong in the appendix.
- Claude should **not use fundraise-deck criteria** to audit a structurer proposal. A proposal that "lacks a why-now slide" or "doesn't address the market size" is correctly formatted as a structurer proposal — not failing as a fundraise deck. Audit it against the rubric in §7.
- Claude should **not omit events of default or garantias language.** These are not optional disclosures — they are the core risk-protection mechanism for both the issuer and investors. A structurer proposal without them is incomplete.
- Claude should **not confuse `[YOUR FIRM]`, `[ISSUER]`, and `[ASSET CLASS]` placeholders with actual deal specifics.** All three must be filled in before the proposal is distributed. If Claude doesn't have the counterparty name, the instrument type, or the firm name, it should ask — not invent.
